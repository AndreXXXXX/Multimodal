McCarthyism


The start

The paranoia of a communist threat reached its peak during the early stages of the cold war, in an event known as the second red scare. Senator Mccarthy was largely responsible for this, through accusing people of having ties to communism and repressing them. His method later became known as Mccarthyism, which is "the practice of making false or unfounded accusations of subversion and treason, especially when related to anarchism, communism and socialism, and especially when done in a public and attention-grabbing manner."

Important figures such as Leonard Bernstein, Aaron Copland, and even Martin Luther King Jr. were accused of being communists.

Counterattack: The Newsletter of Facts on Communism


The Media

Newsletters such as Counterattack: The Newsletter of Facts on Communism, started by 3 ex-FBI agents, targeted people in all industries, but most notably the entertainment industries. Any people accused of having ties to communism or the Soviets were blacklisted from jobs and expelled from society, no matter the validity of the claims. Companies even began hiring special employees to run background checks on potential hires.

The Cold War

The Red Scare provides a glimpse into how the media operates


Fearmongering

The second red scare is perhaps the clearest example of this tactic being used. The media heighted paranoia and fear, causing citizens to develop extreme views.


Pseudo-event

The second red scare should've never happened. It was an event crafted by McCarthy and the media with the goal of providing Americans with a moral high ground. There was no threat of a communist takeover, yet the media still managed to publicize a situation to the point where people were being blacklisted from jobs for no real reason.


Echo Chamber

The media provided people with what they wanted to hear, and they believed it all. People began to expect certain content from the media, and the media had to provide them with that content, even if they had to make it up.


The Effect of Media on Public Opinion


In both the past and the present, the media has a significant effect on public opinion in the United States. It is to the point that public opinion and media opinion are almost always the same. In modern times, this is evident, with the current political polarization being a direct effect of this. On foreign affairs, also, the media has not changed much from how it used to operate during the Cold War and the Red Scare.
